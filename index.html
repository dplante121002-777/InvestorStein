MensRx™ & WomensRx™ – Accredited Investor Cockpit

This page is Glen’s on-screen cockpit for a 20–60 minute call with a serious, skeptical accredited investor. Think Mark Cuban’s bluntness plus Andrew Dudum’s operator brain. The goal is not to “sound impressive” – it’s to survive hard questions, stay credible, and make the $250K seed decision feel logical, not emotional.

Brutal truth

What this investor actually wants to know

No BS
  • 1. Is the problem big, real, and painful – or just another wellness buzzword?
  • 2. Are you actually different from Hims/Hers – or just a smaller, riskier copy?
  • 3. Do you personally have the background to pull this off without blowing up?
  • 4. What happens with my $250K – line by line – and how quickly do you de-risk it?

Back in 2017, long before the current telehealth wave, I secured the MensRx and WomensRx trademarks because I saw where this category was heading. Our goal is to build the trusted clinical vitality brands for both men and women in the U.S., de-risk the model in the first 12 to 18 months, and position this as a platform that can either scale independently or be acquired once the economics are proven.

Hard / Brutal Questions – Cuban Mode

These are the ones that can rattle Glen. The key is to stay calm, admit risk, and show discipline.

INVESTOR: “Why hasn’t someone deeper and better funded already done this and won?”
Glen: “Because doing deep clinical work at scale is hard. Most strong clinical teams move slowly and don’t build great growth machines. Most great growth teams cut corners on the medical side to move fast. Our advantage is that this is what I’ve done for decades – building provider networks, telehealth systems, and pharmacy relationships that can operate across states without tripping over compliance. That doesn’t guarantee success, but it does mean we’re not naive about the operational complexity here.”
INVESTOR: “What if this ends up being a super expensive experiment?”
Glen: “Then we’ll call it what it is. If we run the 12–18 month plan, follow the data, and the unit economics aren’t there – acquisition costs are too high, retention is too low – then your $250,000 bought clarity in a big category instead of returns. I’d rather be upfront about that possibility than sell you a fantasy. The flip side is, if the numbers are good, you’re in early at a level that makes the upside meaningful.”
INVESTOR: “How likely is it that this is a 10x, 20x outcome versus a 0?”
Glen: “The space itself clearly has 10x and 20x potential – Hims and Hers proved that. The question is whether our specific execution earns a meaningful slice of that. We’re not promising a guaranteed 20x. We’re saying: this is a big, validated category where a clinical-depth, dual-gender, compliance-first player can be very valuable if we hit our milestones. If that risk-reward profile fits how you like to invest, then this is worth serious consideration.”

Traps to Avoid – Things Glen Must Not Say

Glen: If you feel defensive, slow down. Do not say any of the following.
  • “We’re the first…”
    You’re not. Hims/Hers exist. Always position on depth, not first-ness.
  • “There’s basically no risk…”
    That’s how you lose credibility instantly. Always acknowledge execution risk.
  • Over-promising CAC or LTV numbers you don’t have.
    Use language like “we’re going to learn the real numbers and scale based on what’s actually working.”
  • Trashing Hims/Hers.
    Respect them as proof of demand; differentiate calmly.
  • Getting lost in medical jargon.
    Keep explanations at the “smart layperson” level. Investors care more about risk, economics, and your judgment.
